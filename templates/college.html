College Analytics

Big Data Predictive Analytics and Exploratory Statistical Analysis

Colleen Karnas-Haines, PhD

Project Profile

This project looks at data from four-year colleges collected from the Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (ipeds) found here and the US Department of Education's College Scorecard found here.

With seven thousand colleges and hundreds of variables collected over decades, this Big Data project required extensive data cleaning and data exploration. Before any data cleaning could take place, I had to learn how each variable was defined. For example, if a school reported a 60% graduation rate, does that mean 60% of their students graduated in 4 yrs? 6 yrs? At some undefined point in their lives?

To successfully predict trends and outcomes, the sample colleges need to be alike in type--similar accreditation, degree-types, and federal Title IV funding eligibility. This study is limited to colleges and universities in the United States that primarily offer baccalaureate degrees, with or without additional graduate degrees. Two-year community colleges, trade schools, and gradute degree-only schools, such as medical schools, were omitted to ensure the predictive analysis was meaningful. Public, private non profit, and private for profit colleges were all included in the analysis. Analyzing the data by a college's tax status proved enlightening.

Public

Public colleges use both state funds and tuition funds to cover education costs and they do not pay taxes on their profits. While there are other sources of income such as endowments and grants, these other sources of income do not affect the classification of the college.

Private, non profit

Private non profit colleges do not receive state funding and use tuition funds to cover education costs. They do not pay taxes on their income. While there are other sources of income such as endowments and grants, these other sources of income do not affect the classification of the college.

Private, for profit

Private for profit colleges do not receive state funding and use tuition funds to cover education costs. They are required to pay taxes on their income. How do we find variables that will accurately predict success?

Once we find variables that correlate to success metrics, can we make predictions?

Finding correlations between variables is a task not always suited to human logic. I did not always find correlations (enrollment measures and revenue, for example) where I thought they should be. Taking advantage of my python programming skills, I built a function that explores one dependent variable and loops through and runs a linear regression analysison hundreds of independent variables. After calculating the coefficient of determination, R^2 and Mean Squared Error (MSE)on each dependent/independent variable pair, the function built a pandas dataframe ordered by the R^2. Then the program took the top three R-squared variables, and combined them into a multivariable linear regression to see if we increase the dependent/independent variables' correlation.

Additionally, I was interested in predictive analytics. Specifically, I wanted to know if one could predict if a college would increase or decrease their yearly revenue and enrollment figures. To investigate these questions I used scikit-learn's KNN analysis. This machine learning technique is commonly known as a nearest neighbor analysis. Multiple variables are used to plot the given data in a multi-planed space. Then, the user can enter in unique data, or in this case, data drawn from a specific college, and the computer analyzes the category of k-neighbors, k determined by best results, to predict what category the user-entered school will fall in. Our categories were increasing revenue vs decreasing revenue and increasing enrollments vs decreasing enrollments.
